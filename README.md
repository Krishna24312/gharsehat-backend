# GharSehat 🩹
- The app is deployed at https://gharsehat.onrender.com.

**A computer-vision-powered burn wound recovery companion for Indian home caregivers — it helps surface day-to-day visual changes in wound photos and prioritizes cases for doctor review.**

Built for techspire hackathon.

> ⚠️ **GharSehat detects measurable *visual change*, never a diagnosis.** Every output describes change only, such as *"redness area increased"* or *"visual change detected."* It never claims infection, necrosis, pus, slough, wound depth, or any medical condition. It is **not a medical diagnosis** and is no substitute for a doctor.

---

## The problem

After a burn patient is discharged, recovery happens at home. A family caregiver — often with no medical training — has to judge, day to day, whether the wound is healing or getting worse. Small changes are hard to see by eye, clinics may be far, and by the time something looks obviously wrong it may already be serious.

Informal photo-sharing over messaging apps is also hard for doctors to review systematically. Photos may be scattered, symptoms may be missing, and there is often no structured timeline of how the wound changed over multiple days.

---

## The idea

The caregiver photographs the wound each day. GharSehat compares today's photo with yesterday's, measures **visual change**, combines it with a short symptom checklist, and gives the caregiver a clear Hindi/English verdict and next step.

At the same time, the completed check-in is saved into a doctor portal, where doctors can review patients in risk order, inspect the day-wise timeline, and expand advanced non-diagnostic photo-analysis details when needed.

Two core safety principles drive the design:

1. **Detect change, never diagnose.**
2. **Prioritize sensitivity over precision** — in this prototype, a cautious false alarm is preferable to missing a possible deterioration.

---

## What it does

### Caregiver app

* **Guided photo capture** with a live camera quality check for lighting, blur, and framing.
* **Upload fallback** for controlled demo images or when camera capture is unreliable.
* **Symptom checklist** covering fever, smell, spreading redness, discharge, and increasing pain.
* **Instant plain-language result** with:

  * headline guidance,
  * reason summary,
  * next step,
  * care-level meter,
  * photo-comparison summary,
  * safety disclaimer.
* **Bilingual support** in Hindi and English.
* **Fixed medical message templates** — caregiver-facing advice is not generated by an LLM.
* **Escalation guidance** from stable/watch closely to show doctor today or call 108.

### Doctor portal

* **Risk-sorted patient list** — red first, then amber, then green.
* **Per-patient recovery timeline** with day-wise history.
* **Score trend** for quick longitudinal review.
* **Wound photo strip** showing hardcoded demo history and submitted check-in photos.
* **Submitted yesterday/today photos** from caregiver check-ins.
* **Latest symptoms and scores** for doctor review.
* **Advanced photo analysis** shown as a collapsed section for doctors only.
* **Live updates by polling** — submitted check-ins appear in the portal without automated push notifications.

## How the scoring works

A check-in produces a **change score** from the photo comparison and a **symptom score** from the checklist.

```text
symptom weights:
spreading_redness = 30
fever = 25
discharge = 20
smell = 15
increasing_pain = 10

symptom_score = sum of weights for symptoms marked true

final_score = change_score × 0.5 + symptom_score × 0.5
```

`final_score` maps to a status and caregiver action:

| final_score | status   | caregiver action       |
| ----------- | -------- | ---------------------- |
| `< 30`      | 🟢 green | Continue care          |
| `30–59`     | 🟡 amber | Watch closely          |
| `60–74`     | 🔴 red   | Show doctor today      |
| `≥ 75`      | 🔴 red   | Call 108 / urgent care |

The numeric scores remain available for doctor review, but the caregiver result screen emphasizes plain-language guidance rather than raw numbers.

## Photo comparison layer

GharSehat has two photo-analysis modes.

### 1. `/analyze` — demo/mock analysis

This endpoint returns a fixed, reliable visual-change score.

It is intentionally kept as a **demo safety net** so the app remains usable even if lighting, camera permissions, or image quality are poor during a live demo.

### 2. `/analyze-real` — real OpenCV analysis

This endpoint runs an experimental OpenCV-based photo comparison.

The pipeline:

1. Decodes yesterday and today images.
2. Resizes the photos for consistent processing.
3. Crops the central region of interest.
4. Runs multi-threshold HSV redness analysis.
5. Keeps only thresholds showing positive redness growth.
6. Uses the median of valid redness-growth thresholds.
7. Computes supporting non-diagnostic visual metrics:

   * dark visual area change,
   * yellow/cream visual area change,
   * approximate visual-region area change,
   * combined border change.
8. Returns visual-change values and debug information.

The real CV pipeline is designed for **serial comparison under guided capture**, not one-off diagnosis from arbitrary wound photos.

Important limitations:

* no scale normalization,
* no lighting normalization,
* no true wound segmentation,
* no depth assessment,
* no diagnosis of infection, necrosis, pus, slough, or tissue type.

The advanced visual metrics are shown only in the doctor portal and are collapsed by default to avoid overwhelming caregivers.

## Doctor-side advanced photo analysis

When a real OpenCV check-in is saved, the doctor portal can show a collapsed **Advanced photo analysis** section.

Example labels:

```text
Red visual area: High
Dark visual area: Mild
Yellow/cream visual area: Low
Approx. visual-region change: Mild
Combined border change: Low

Non-diagnostic visual features for doctor review.
```

The portal intentionally shows severity labels rather than raw pixel/debug counts. These metrics are supporting signals for review, not diagnostic outputs.

## Responsible-AI and clinical safety guardrails

GharSehat is not a medical device and is not clinically validated.

It does not diagnose:

```text
infection
necrosis
pus
slough
wound depth
burn severity
clinical deterioration
```

It uses safe wording:

```text
visual change
photo comparison
symptoms reported
doctor review
urgent care recommended
non-diagnostic visual features
```

Guardrails implemented:

* caregiver-facing advice uses fixed templates,
* no LLM-generated medical advice,
* Hindi messages use Devanagari,
* every result includes a medical disclaimer,
* advanced photo metrics are doctor-side and collapsed by default,
* mock analysis remains available as a demo safety fallback,
* doctor portal receives submitted check-ins but no automated doctor notification is claimed.

## Limitations

Current limitations include:

* not clinically validated,
* not production-ready,
* no authentication,
* no database-backed persistence,
* no secure cloud image storage,
* local JSON/file storage only,
* no scale/reference-card normalization,
* no lighting normalization,
* no true wound boundary segmentation,
* no wound area calibration,
* no confirmed epithelialization/healing percentage,
* no clinical diagnosis of tissue type or infection,
* OpenCV outputs may be affected by shadows, dressings, skin tone, background, angle, and camera distance,
* advanced visual features are supporting signals only,
* real-world performance requires validation on de-identified clinical image sequences.

---

## Scientific and clinical motivation

Digital wound analysis and planimetry approaches can estimate healing-related parameters using pixel areas when wound boundaries and open wound regions are identified. However, reliable assessment often requires clinician judgment or manual marking of wound regions, especially because wound tissues vary in appearance and are difficult to distinguish using color alone.

GharSehat currently implements an early automated visual-change layer and treats all outputs as non-diagnostic. Future versions could add clinician-guided wound boundary confirmation and validated wound-area tracking.
